Datastory: Socioeconomic inequality#

Introduction#

Socioeconomic inequality stands as a common issue that steeps societies worldwide, generating disparities in wealth and opportunities. To unravel the underlying causes of this inequality, we dive into two key perspectives: The impact of race and the influence of poverty on socioeconomic inequality. Poverty refers to a lack of income and resources to meet basic needs, affecting individuals across different racial backgrounds. By analysing these perspectives, we uncover how different factors contribute to the unequal distribution of resources and opportunities within our society. Ultimately to gain a comprehensive understanding of the correlations between socioeconomic inequality and these two factors.

Dataset and preprocessing#

The first dataset is called ‘Offenders Race by Offense Category’. The dataset can be found here: https://cde.ucr.cjis.gov/LATEST/webapp/#/pages/downloads. The dataset ‘Offenders Race by Offense Category’ contains data of different types of crime activities by their race in 2021. The variables are total offenders, white, black or African American, American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian and Native Hawaiian or unknown race. It can be used for data analysis between the correlation of crime and race. The preprocessing steps for this dataset involved converting Excel files to CSV format using Python’s pandas library. This conversation was made to achieve easier data handling and analysis. Additionally, the data consisted of many categories of offences. In order to ensure simplicity and relevance, the data has been stripped down to only the Total offences category.

The second dataset is “Educational Attainment in the United States: 2021”, sourced from the U.S. Census Bureau and offers valuable insights into educational achievements among various racial groups in the United States. The dataset can be accessed through the link: https://www.census.gov/data/tables/2021/demo/educational-attainment/cps-detailed-tables.html. This dataset gives us information on the educational attainment levels of individuals belonging to different racial backgrounds in 2021. The dataset includes data on educational milestones such as completion of high school, attainment of bachelor’s degrees and engagement in higher education levels. This dataset focuses on the racial groups White-non hispanic, Black, Asian and Hispanics. The preprocessing steps for this dataset involved converting Excel files to CSV format using Python’s pandas library. This conversation was made to achieve easier data handling and analysis. The dataset includes age and sex variables. To ensure readability and simplicity, analysis focuses on individuals aged 18 years and older. For educational attainment, the focus is on achievements beyond high school graduate level. Lastly, the values are given in thousands, which is converted with the use of the pandas library.

The third dataset is a subset for National Mortality Rates (Table 15), sourced from The Health Inequality Project which can be accessed through the link: https://www.healthinequality.org/data/. This dataset provides information on mortality rates in the US by gender, age, year and household income percentile. The household income percentiles are calculated independently for each gender, age group and year.
The following procedures were applied to work with the data. Initially, a series of columns were removed until only the percentile and the mortality rates columns remained. The remaining data was grouped based on percentiles. Each column represented a 20 percentile range and the average value was computed for each group.

The fourth dataset is “Fatal Encounters” and can be found at: https://fatalencounters.org/. This dataset focuses on documenting and providing information on fatal encounters involving law enforcement officers in the United States. This dataset gives us information on incidents of fatalities related to law enforcement encounters including date, location, race, circumstances surrounding each case. Allowing researchers to analyse trends, patterns and potential biases in the use of force by law enforcement. To work with the data of “Fatal Encounters” everything except the variables, the amount of fatalities and the races. Then the frequency per racial group was taken to visualise the data more optimally.

The fifth dataset is ‘Graduation Rate’ and can be found here: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/rkiattisak/graduation-rate This dataset is useful for investigating how much influence parental education and income have on a child’s school performance. It contains the child’s ACT score, SAT score, high school GPA, college GPA, years to graduate, parental income, level of education. While working with the data the only variables that were left out are the child’s high school GPA and years to graduate.
